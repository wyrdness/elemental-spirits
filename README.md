## Overview

Elemental spirits, or 'elementals', are mythic supernatural beings systematized in the alchemical and occult writings of the Swiss physician Paracelsus (1493/4–1541), who organized them into four classes corresponding to the classical Empedoclean elements: gnomes (earth), undines or nymphs (water), sylphs (air), and salamanders (fire). Paracelsus described them not as pure spirits but as intermediate beings — invisible yet possessing humanoid bodies, capable of eating, sleeping, and clothing themselves — and held that they lacked an immortal soul, but could acquire one through marriage with a human being. His foundational text 'A Book on Nymphs, Sylphs, Pygmies, and Salamanders, and on the Other Spirits' (Liber de Nymphis, sylphis, pygmaeis et salamandris et de caeteris spiritibus), part of the Philosophia Magna, was first printed in 1566 after his death. The framework was elaborated by Heinrich Cornelius Agrippa (De Occulta Philosophia, 1531–1533) and dramatically popularized by the Abbé de Montfaucon de Villars's 1670 satirical work 'Comte de Gabalis', which inspired Alexander Pope's 'The Rape of the Lock' (1712) and Friedrich de la Motte Fouqué's romantic novella 'Undine' (1811). Modern Theosophical, Rosicrucian, and Wiccan currents continue to invoke elementals as spirits of nature; the framework also pervades fantasy fiction and games, including 'Dungeons & Dragons'.

## Etymology

- **Origin**: Latin elementalis ('elemental'), from elementum, applied as a noun to nature spirits in 16th- and 17th-century occult writings.
- **Meaning**: A spirit or being of, and confined to, one of the four classical elements: earth, water, air, or fire.
- **First use**: Mid-16th century in Paracelsus; the Latin terms gnomus, undina, and sylph appear to originate in his works.

## Theories & Explanations

### Paranormal / Cryptozoological

**Paracelsian elemental ontology** _(Paracelsus, Heinrich Cornelius Agrippa, Rosicrucian tradition, H. P. Blavatsky (Theosophy))_

There exist four real classes of intermediate beings, bound to and constituted by the four classical elements; these beings interact with humans and may be perceived by purified senses.

**Theosophical / contemporary Pagan view** _(H. P. Blavatsky, C. W. Leadbeater, Modern Wiccan practitioners)_

Elementals are ensouled or semi-ensouled beings stewarding aspects of the natural world; they are participants in ecological and energetic processes.

### Skeptical

**Anthropomorphization of natural phenomena** _(Folklorists and historians of science)_

Elementals are pre-modern attempts to anthropomorphize physical processes (weather, fire, geology, water) within an animistic framework that has been superseded by natural science.

**Literary and esoteric construction** _(Historians of esotericism (e.g. Wouter Hanegraaff))_

Paracelsus's elemental system is a synthesis of classical sources, folklore, and theology produced in the context of Renaissance philosophical reform; later 'sightings' are products of literary and esoteric tradition rather than independent observation.
